I'm a Machine Learning Engineer at Booz Allen Hamilton, where I lead a team of five engineers building LLM-powered systems for Department of Defense clients. I hold dual M.S. degrees in Applied Mathematics & Statistics (Stony Brook) and Physics (Seton Hall), and have worked across Booz Allen, RAND Corporation, the FBI, and Brookhaven National Laboratory.

My independent research asks a single recurring question: **when should you trust what a model has learned?** Across reinforcement learning theory, scientific ML, epidemiological inference, and training dynamics, I study how structural assumptions — coverage, architecture, observation bias — determine whether learned or inferred signals are reliable. I develop methods for diagnosing when they aren't.

## Recent Work

In early 2026 I posted three solo arXiv preprints:

- [**Information Routing in Atomistic Foundation Models via Composition Projection Decomposition**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03155) — a new decomposition method (CPD) showing that different atomistic neural network architectures route compositional information in fundamentally different ways, with downstream consequences for what representations encode. Accompanied by [MatScope](https://pypi.org/project/matscope/), an open-source Python library.
- [**A Survey of PAC-Guaranteed Reinforcement Learning**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.17457) — a systematic survey organizing PAC-RL theory through a Coverage–Structure–Objective framework.
- [**Contrastive Forward-Forward with Margin Clamping**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.01966) — studying how margin clamping interacts with network width, dataset difficulty, and gradient variance in biologically plausible learning.

I'm also a maintainer of [PyHealth](https://github.com/sunlabuiuc/PyHealth), an open-source clinical ML library, where I contributed the graph-based modeling module and am building out the GraphCare implementation.

## Research Themes

My [research page](/research/) groups my work by the questions driving it rather than by domain. The short version: I keep finding that signals which look meaningful under one set of assumptions become unreliable or invert under another — whether the "signal" is a molecular property extracted from a neural network, a policy guarantee in RL, or an apparent epidemic interaction confounded by shared testing.

## Background

Before my current research focus, I spent two years at RAND Corporation working on ML safety, cybersecurity, and climate AI (awarded the RAND Innovation Award for bringing MLOps to the organization), and earlier built NLP and graph-based detection tools at Brookhaven National Laboratory and the FBI. I've taught at Stony Brook University (Mathematical Biology), and have 15+ publications spanning ML, epidemiology, physics, and graph theory.
